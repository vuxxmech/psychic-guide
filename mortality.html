09 / 09 — Death · Metaphysics

The Shadow
Before Birth

A reading of roughly 8 minutes

Epicurus offered what he believed was a decisive argument against the fear of death. "Death is nothing to us," he wrote, "since when we are, death has not come, and when death has come, we are not." The argument is elegant. There is no moment at which both you and your death coexist — at every moment you are either alive or gone. There is no subject left to suffer the absence.

The argument is logically sound. It has persuaded almost no one.

This asymmetry — between what philosophy can prove and what the mind can actually feel — is itself philosophically interesting. Why does a valid argument fail to produce the equanimity it promises? The answer is not simply irrationality. The fear of death, examined carefully, turns out to track something real that Epicurus's argument does not address.

The Prenatal Non-Existence

The philosopher Thomas Nagel drew attention to what he called the asymmetry between our attitudes toward post-mortem non-existence and prenatal non-existence. For thirteen-plus billion years before you were born, you did not exist. Most people do not regard this as a misfortune. They do not mourn the experiences they missed, the centuries of human history they were absent for, the countless lives that were lived without them.

Yet the comparable period after death — the same quality of non-existence, the same absence of experience — is typically regarded as a profound loss. If non-existence is not bad before birth, why is it bad after death?

One answer (associated with Lucretius, who repeated and expanded Epicurus's argument) is that this reaction is simply an inconsistency — a quirk of temporal perspective that has no rational justification. We are familiar with the time before us; we are less familiar with the time after. The asymmetry in feeling reflects only the direction of our experience, not any real difference between the two voids.

"Look back at the eternity that passed before we were born, and mark how utterly it counts to us as nothing. This is a mirror that Nature holds up to us, in which we may see the time that shall be after our death."

— Lucretius, De Rerum Natura, c. 50 BCE

The Deprivation Account

The deprivation account of death's badness, associated most recently with Thomas Nagel and Jeff McMahan, argues that what makes death bad is not any future suffering but the goods it deprives you of. Death cuts short a life that would otherwise have contained value — experiences, relationships, achievements, pleasures. The harm of death is the gap between the life you would have lived and the life you did live.

This account survives Epicurus's argument. It does not require that you experience your death as bad. It only requires that your death makes things worse than they otherwise would have been — and that claim is evaluated relative to the subject who existed before death, not the non-subject who does not exist afterward.

But the deprivation account opens a different vertigo. It implies that the goodness of being alive is constituted, in part, by the badness of death — that life derives meaning partly from the fact that it ends. And it implies that the harm of death scales with the life that would have been lived. A long, rich life with decades of future value ahead is harmed more by death than a short or impoverished one. Youth, on this account, is maximally vulnerable.

The Non-Identity Problem

Derek Parfit's non-identity problem deepens the disorientation. Many things that affect whether a particular person exists are not, on the face of it, harms or benefits to that person — because the person whose existence is affected would not otherwise exist to be harmed or benefited.

Had your parents met one year later, or had your conception occurred on a different night, a different person would have existed — or no one. The you that exists is constituted by a precise set of contingent events. This means you are lucky to exist at all in a very particular sense: the circumstances that produced you were not inevitable. A different set of circumstances would have produced someone else, or no one. There is no metaphysical pressure that guaranteed your particular existence.

You are, in the deepest sense, an accident. A specific outcome of a vast probability space in which most outcomes do not include you. The shadow before birth is not merely the absence of experience — it is the almost impossible narrowness of the path that led to there being a "you" to have experience at all.

Mortality as Horizon

Martin Heidegger argued that mortality is not merely a fact about human beings but constitutive of their mode of being. We are, he said, beings-toward-death. The awareness of finitude structures temporality, gives urgency to choice, and makes authenticity possible — because authenticity requires acknowledging what one actually is, and what one actually is includes the certainty of ending.

On Heidegger's account, what most people call "life" is a form of flight from this awareness. Das Man — the anonymous "they," the social mass — provides a comfortable world in which death is "known about" in a general, impersonal way but not faced as a personal, ownmost possibility. Living authentically means dragging death out of the comfortable impersonal and placing it squarely at the centre of one's self-understanding.

Whether this produces equanimity or dread depends on the person. Heidegger thought it could produce something like clarity. Many who have tried it have found the opposite: that looking directly at death does not defuse the fear but intensifies it — strips away the buffers and leaves the fact standing there, undisguised.

The Argument That Does Not Help

Epicurus's argument is valid. You will not be there to experience the loss. The pre-mortem you who fears death will not persist into the post-mortem void to suffer it. The absence is complete.

And yet: you are here now, reading this, knowing that this — all of this — will one day not be. The world will continue, indifferent, and the particular configuration of matter that generates your awareness will disperse, and there will be no record anywhere that it felt anything.

Epicurus is right that there will be no one to mourn this. The horror is that the mourning has to happen in advance.
