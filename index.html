The Unanswerable

Questions With No Exit

Philosophy's deepest tradition is not providing answers but staying honestly with the questions. These five have been open for millennia.

Why is there something rather than nothing?

Leibniz first posed this in 1714. It remains unanswered. Every scientific explanation of existence presupposes something existing. The question tunnels beneath all explanation.

Could there be a mind that experiences nothing?

The philosophical zombie thought experiment asks us to imagine a being physically identical to you in every way but with no inner life. We cannot prove such a being does not exist — or that you are not one, watching a self you cannot access.

Is the past real?

Physics gives us a block universe: past, present, and future all equally real, equally fixed. Your memory of yesterday is not a connection to a living past — it is a representation in the present of a slice of spacetime that is permanent and unreachable.

What is the self, if not a story?

Hume looked inward and found only a "bundle of perceptions" — no central self, only a stream of experiences narrating a continuity that may not exist. The self as author may be indistinguishable from the self as fiction.

Does meaning require permanence?

Camus argued that recognising the absurd — the collision of our demand for meaning and the universe's silence — need not end in despair. But the recognition itself is the first step into a room with no obvious exit.
