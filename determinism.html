02 / 06 — Determinism · Free Will

The Clockwork
& The Ghost

A reading of roughly 9 minutes

Right now, as you read this, you feel as though you are choosing to continue — or choosing to stop. You feel the weight of decision, the sense that multiple futures lie ahead of you and that you are the one who will determine which one obtains. This feeling is so pervasive, so constant, that it is almost invisible. It is the water you swim in.

It may be an illusion.

The argument for determinism is not complex. Every physical event — including every brain state — is caused by prior physical events, which are themselves caused by prior physical events, all the way back to initial conditions at the beginning of the universe. If this is true, then the thought you are having right now was, in principle, determined at the Big Bang. Not by a god, not by fate — but by physics. Blind, indifferent, mechanical.

The Standard Responses

There are three standard responses to this argument, and each has a problem.

The first is hard determinism: free will is simply an illusion, and we should accept this and revise our moral, legal, and personal concepts accordingly. This position is honest, but it generates consequences most people find unacceptable — including the conclusion that no one is ever genuinely responsible for anything they do.

The second is libertarian free will (not in the political sense): the soul, or some non-physical aspect of the self, can intervene in the causal chain and introduce genuine choice. This preserves intuitions about responsibility but at enormous cost — it requires a violation of the physical causal closure of the universe and offers no mechanism for how a non-physical agent interacts with a physical brain.

The third is compatibilism: free will and determinism are not in conflict because "free will" properly understood just means acting according to your own desires and reasoning without external coercion — not some magical capacity to violate causation. This is the position of most professional philosophers, and it is intellectually defensible.

"A man can do what he wills, but he cannot will what he wills."

— Arthur Schopenhauer, Essay on the Freedom of the Will, 1839

What Compatibilism Does Not Resolve

Compatibilism is philosophically careful, but it has a strange quality of changing the subject. When people worry about free will, they are typically asking whether they are the ultimate originators of their actions — whether the buck truly stops with them. Compatibilism responds by pointing out that their actions do flow from their character, their desires, their reasoning. True. But those too were determined. The compatibilist definition of freedom does not touch the real source of anxiety.

Schopenhauer's formulation captures this precisely. You can do what you will — but you cannot choose what you will. Your desires, your values, your deliberative capacities themselves — these are products of your genetics, your upbringing, your experiences, your neurology. You did not choose any of those. The self that deliberates is itself the product of causes it did not choose.

The Neuroscience

In the 1980s, Benjamin Libet conducted experiments measuring brain activity during voluntary movements. He found something striking: the "readiness potential" — the brain activity associated with initiating a movement — preceded the subject's conscious awareness of intending to move by approximately half a second. The decision had been made, at a neural level, before the subject was aware of deciding.

Libet's experiments have been criticised, replicated, refined, and extended over forty years. The precise interpretation remains contested. But the general picture they support — that the conscious experience of deciding is a downstream report on a process that has already occurred, rather than the cause of that process — has become more, not less, credible over time.

The philosopher Daniel Dennett has argued that we can still maintain a meaningful sense of authorship over our actions even on this picture. Others, like Sam Harris, have argued that the implications are more radical — that the sense of being a free agent is a story the brain tells itself, systematically and continuously, about events that were never in its control.

The Weight of This

There is a peculiar quality to sitting with this seriously. Everything you have ever done — the moments you are proud of, the ones you regret, the choices that made you — unrolled according to a necessity that was laid down before you were born. The love you feel for the people closest to you was assembled by forces outside your control. The person you became was never really up to you.

Some people find this liberating. It dissolves guilt, punctures pride, softens judgment. Others find it annihilating — a kind of ontological vertigo in which the floor of the self falls away.

Quantum mechanics introduces irreducible randomness into the universe, which at first seems to rescue free will. But randomness is not freedom. If your choices are determined by chance rather than your character, that is not autonomy — it is noise. The quantum exit from determinism does not lead back to the self.

The View From Here

Philosophers have been debating this for millennia. The debate is not close to ending. What we can say is this: the naïve picture — the picture of a self standing outside the causal order and genuinely choosing among open futures — is very difficult to defend with rigour. What replaces it is something stranger, more textured, less comforting, and possibly more honest.

You are reading these words. Something in you is processing them. What that something is, and whether it is in any meaningful sense free, remains one of the deepest open questions in philosophy. What is not open is the question of whether it matters. It matters immensely — which is precisely why the question refuses to close.
