The Last Light

A reading of roughly 8 minutes

The universe has a direction. This is not metaphor. It is not philosophy. It is physics. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that in any isolated system, entropy — disorder, the number of possible microstates — tends to increase over time. The universe is, by definition, an isolated system. Its entropy has been increasing since the Big Bang, and it will continue increasing until there is nothing left for it to increase toward.

This endpoint has a name: the heat death of the universe. Maximum entropy. A cold, dark, uniform expanse in which no structure exists, no gradients remain, no work can be done. No stars. No planets. No minds. No difference between one place and another. No events. Time, in any meaningful sense, stops.

The Arrow of Time

The Second Law explains something that has puzzled physicists for a century: why time has a direction. At the level of fundamental physics, most processes are time-symmetric — the equations work equally well run forward or backward. There is nothing in the basic laws that distinguishes past from future. And yet you experience time as moving in one direction, irreversibly, and you know the difference between a cup falling off a table and a cup reassembling itself from shards.

The asymmetry comes from entropy. The past has lower entropy than the present. The reason we remember the past and not the future is that the past is a record — a trace of lower-entropy states. Memory is physically possible because the past was more ordered. The future is not yet a record because it has not yet increased in entropy relative to now.

Time's arrow points toward disorder. Everything you experience as narrative — cause following effect, intentions producing outcomes, the progression of a life — is a local eddy of decreasing entropy in a universe whose overall entropy never stops increasing. You are a temporary structure in a river that is always, on the largest scale, running downhill.

"The total amount of suffering per year in the natural world is beyond all decent contemplation... In a universe of electrons and selfish genes, blind physical forces and genetic replication, some people are going to get hurt, other people are going to get lucky, and you won't find any rhyme or reason in it, nor any justice."

— Richard Dawkins, River Out of Eden, 1995

The Timescales

The timescales involved are so large as to strain comprehension. The universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old. The sun will exhaust its hydrogen in about 5 billion years. In approximately 1014 years, all stars will have burned out. In 1040 years, even protons may decay. In 1067 years, stellar-mass black holes will have evaporated via Hawking radiation. In 10100 years, supermassive black holes will be gone.

After that: a cold, dark, near-uniform distribution of weakly interacting particles, separated by unimaginable distances, moving apart forever. No energy differentials large enough to power any process. Nothing to push against. Nothing to organise.

This is not speculation. It is the most rigorously tested prediction in all of science. The Second Law has not failed once across any scale at which it has been tested.

Does It Matter?

The usual response to the heat death of the universe is temporal distance. It is so inconceivably far away that it cannot touch us. This response has a strange quality to it.

Suppose someone told you that the house you were building, and every house after it, and every city that contained houses, and every continent that contained cities, and eventually every planet that contained continents, would one day be completely and permanently destroyed with no trace. You would not take comfort in the fact that this would happen in a trillion years. The certainty of the outcome changes the character of the present, even when the timeline is vast.

The philosopher Albert Camus argued that the confrontation with the absurd — the collision of our need for meaning and the universe's silence — was the fundamental philosophical problem. The heat death takes the absurd and gives it a direction and a terminus. The universe is not merely indifferent to meaning; it is in the process, on the longest timescale, of eliminating all the structures that could instantiate meaning.

The Permanent Past

There is one structural feature of thermodynamics that offers, if not comfort, then at least a strange precision. The past is fixed. The block universe of physics means that events, once occurred, are permanently embedded in the structure of spacetime. Everything that has happened — every moment of joy, every act of understanding, every mind that lit up with awareness — is permanently real as a region of spacetime, even after the matter that composed it has dispersed.

The mathematician and philosopher Frank Tipler proposed, in a spirit of speculative optimism, that sufficiently advanced intelligence might somehow concentrate at the end of the universe and experience infinite subjective time in finite objective time before the end — an "Omega Point" of final consciousness. Almost no physicists take this seriously as physics.

What remains is the shape of the thing: a universe that began in extraordinary order, that generated, in the middle stretch of its history, small pockets of complexity in which matter became aware of itself — and that will end in a darkness so deep that there will be no one left to observe it, and nothing to distinguish one moment from the last.

The last light will go out. Not with violence. Just cooling. Just entropy. Just time, running out.
