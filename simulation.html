The Simulation
& The Real

A reading of roughly 8 minutes

Descartes, in the seventeenth century, decided to doubt everything that could possibly be doubted. The senses, reason, memory — all of it unreliable, all of it potentially deceived by a malicious demon with the power to generate an entirely convincing false reality. The demon hypothesis was a thought experiment, a methodological device. He was not especially worried that it was true.

In 2003, the philosopher Nick Bostrom published a paper that gave Descartes' demon a more precise and considerably more credible form.

The Trilemma

Bostrom's simulation argument is not simply the claim that we might be living in a simulation. It is a structural trilemma. At least one of the following must be true:

First: virtually all civilisations at roughly our level of development go extinct before reaching the capacity to run detailed simulations of minds. Second: virtually all civilisations that reach that capacity choose not to run such simulations — for ethical reasons, or lack of interest, or some other consistent reason. Third: we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation.

The argument is probabilistic. If simulations of minds are possible and are run in large numbers — as they almost certainly would be, given the curiosity and scale of any advanced civilisation — then the simulated minds would vastly outnumber the "base" minds. And you would have no way of knowing which kind you are.

"If there are many more simulated minds than biological minds, you should probably conclude that you are a simulated mind."

— Nick Bostrom, Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?, 2003

Why This Is Not Just Science Fiction

The simulation hypothesis is usually treated as a quirky intellectual diversion — a conversation topic at technology conferences, a premise for films. This treatment understates its seriousness.

The argument's first premise — that sufficiently advanced civilisations could run consciousness-containing simulations — is not obviously false. It does not require magic or supernatural mechanisms. It requires only that consciousness is substrate-independent and that computation can scale arbitrarily. Neither assumption is trivially wrong. In fact, both are taken seriously by many philosophers and physicists.

The second premise — that if such simulations are possible and not universally avoided, many would be run — follows straightforwardly from the nature of knowledge, curiosity, and the economics of computation in advanced civilisations. The histories simulated would vastly outnumber any base reality.

The conclusion then follows from Bayesian reasoning about reference classes. If you are equally likely to be any conscious mind, and simulated minds outnumber non-simulated ones by orders of magnitude, then the probability that you are simulated is correspondingly high.

The Unfalsifiability

What makes the simulation hypothesis philosophically troubling is not the possibility that it is true. It is that it may be genuinely unfalsifiable from the inside. Any evidence you could cite for the "reality" of reality — the coherence of physical law, the reliability of your senses, the consistency of other people's reports — would be equally present in a sufficiently detailed simulation.

Some physicists have proposed that we might detect simulation artefacts — pixelation at the Planck scale, unexpected cutoffs in high-energy cosmic rays — but these proposals depend on assumptions about how the simulation is run that a sufficiently sophisticated simulation could trivially avoid.

Karl Popper's criterion for scientific knowledge was falsifiability: a theory is scientific if it could, in principle, be shown to be false. The simulation hypothesis is structured so that no possible evidence could definitively refute it. This places it outside of science — not as pseudoscience, but as metaphysics in the original sense: a question about what lies beneath the empirical order.

What Would Change If It Were True

Here is the question that seems to matter most: if you discovered, with certainty, that you were living in a simulation — what would change?

Your experiences would still be real experiences. Pain would still hurt. Love would still bind. The sun would still set in the same colours. The people around you would still be present in whatever sense they are present now. The value of things does not seem obviously to depend on the substrate that instantiates them.

And yet something would shift. The sense that the world has depth — that behind appearances lies something solid, something ultimate — would be replaced by the awareness that behind appearances lies only more computation, all the way down, running on hardware you cannot perceive, maintained by entities you cannot contact, for purposes you cannot know.

Descartes solved his demon problem by arguing that God would not allow such deception. That exit is no longer available to most people. What remains is the possibility that the most basic assumption underlying every other assumption — that the world is real — is one we cannot verify and may, by the structure of the argument, never be able to.

The simulation hypothesis is not a claim. It is an aperture. Once opened, it is very difficult to close.
